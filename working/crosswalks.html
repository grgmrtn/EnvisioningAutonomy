Sharing your Crosswalks

Not all autonomous vehicles will use roads like cars. Many of them - like delivery robots - will be smaller. Sidewalks are already too narrow. Do we have room to share with these devices?

↓

Delivery Robots

You may have seen robots like this around Toronto. They're about the size of a suitcase with enough storage room for a food order or a small package.

Do they know how crossing signals work? Won't they just be a danger?

The robots you see on the street today are remotely controlled by a human. We need to develop a system that allows the autonomous versions a safe method of crossing.

The Crosswalk

We don't get much time at crosswalks. A piece of equipment called a signal head displays two things: an icon indicating whether the pedestrian should stop or go, and a countdown before the phase changes again.

The problem is that these suitcase-sized robots may not be able to see the signal, placed at least nine feet off the ground. We need a new type of signal that communicates to sidewalk-using autonomous appliances and to human pedestrians.

This signal head is designed to sit next to the crossing button, much more easily read by autonomous appliances shorter than three feet.

This signal matches the crossing phases of the pedestrian signal. Pedestrians read the symbols in the top panel, and the machines read the QR codes to get the same information.

It also warns autonomous appliances of an upcoming crossing, so that they can prepare to move. This keeps pedestrians more aware of these devices' movements, and it keeps the devices aware of the rules of the crossing.

Interventions like these focus on access to information. If every entity can predict the movement of other entities, everybody ends up safer.
